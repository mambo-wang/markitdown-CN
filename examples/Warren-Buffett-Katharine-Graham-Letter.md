# PENSIONS

There are two aspects of the pension cost problem upon which management can have a significant impact: (1) maintaining rational control over pension plan promises to employees and (2) increasing investment returns on pension plan assets.

## The Irreversible Nature of Pension Promises

To control promises rationally, it is necessary to understand the basic arithmetic and practical rules governing pension plans.

The first thing to recognize, with every pension benefit decision, is that you almost certainly are playing for keeps and won't be able to reverse your decision subsequently if it produces subnormal profitability.

In practically every industry, including those with vigorous competition and those with union-management adversarial relationships, it has proved impossible to decrease pension benefits — or even, in most cases, to hold them level once granted. The "out" clauses in plans (giving the company the right to terminate the plan at any time) have proved to be words without meaning. Operating practicalities, as well as legal decisions, have rendered residual rights to terminate moot.

Thus, rule number one regarding pension costs is to know what you are getting into before signing up. In no other area of corporate management is such ignorance so widespread — or has such costly consequences. A major factor has been the failure to think of pension costs in terms similar to those used in evaluating other cost items. This failure has been encouraged by the arcane nature of actuarial thinking, where the lexicon is arcane, the numbers seem unreal, and making promises never quite triggers the visceral response evoked by writing a check.

In no other managerial area are such huge aggregate liabilities reflected in progressively increasing annual costs and cash requirements. As in pressroom labor practices, small errors compound quickly. Care and caution are in order.

## Deceptive Arithmetic of "Promise Now — Pay Later"

If you promise to pay me $500 per month for life, you have just expended — actuarially but, nevertheless, in a totally tangible sense — about $65,000. If you are financially good for such a lifetime promise, you would be better off (if I have an average expectancy regarding longevity for one my age) handing me a check for $50,000. But it wouldn't feel the same.

If you promise to pay me fifty-cent hamburgers once a month for life, you have just laid out about $6,000 by current actuarial assumptions — and if you're good for the promise, you'd be better off handing me $5,000 now (assuming I will live to the statistical age expected for one my age). No one would think of entering into such a contract, which requires him to furnish hamburgers at current prices to someone who bought them years before at fifty-cent prices.

However, such hamburger contracts are analogous to pension obligations denominated in dollars — which you promise to pay in the future as a result of services currently rendered. In a world of price stability, there is no difference. But, in a world of price inflation, the promise to pay a fixed number of dollars is a promise to deliver a diminishing quantity of hamburgers. In a world of high inflation, the purchasing power shrinkage is dramatic.

Few pension plans are completely indexed, but many contain escalator provisions — and most plans have been repeatedly amended upward on an ad hoc basis. Thus, the actual experience of corporations in the private sector has been that their pension obligations have been considerably larger than was contemplated at the time that the promises were made.

In fairness, it must be recognized that this was not entirely a matter of more generous benefits being granted. The benefit levels contemplated at the initiation of many plans would have been fully adequate if the assumptions regarding inflation had been met. But inflation has accelerated — and the consequences have been dramatic.

## Illustrated Elementary Actuarial Principles

Let's look at a highly oversimplified example of the arithmetic. (For the sake of brevity, this example assumes that each employee works to retirement at age 65 — that there is no turnover — and that each employee is a male with a wife of the same age, who will receive a 50% survivor benefit. Obviously, actual plans are much more complex, but the basic principles illustrated here are not altered by such complexity.)

If you have four employees, each 55 years old, who earn $300 per month, and you promise them $300 per month at age 65 (plus $150 per month to their wives if they predecease them), you have just committed yourself to a very large expenditure — even though the annual cost figures which you will see won't look that way.

If we assume that these employees will live to the statistical life expectancy (71 for males, 78 for females), and that money will earn 7% after all expenses, you have just laid out about $70,000. This figure represents the present value of the benefits that will be paid to these four employees and their wives.

In a sense, you have just agreed to set aside about $70,000 now — at 7% interest — to grow to about $140,000 in ten years, which then will be used to purchase annuities providing about $3,600 per year for the retirees (and their surviving spouses) for as long as any of them live.

But the situation looks much different if our employees are earning $600 per month — and we promise them $600 per month at retirement, with a $300 per month survivor benefit. The present value of this promise is approximately $140,000 — double the amount of the previous example. If these same employees earned $1,200 per month and we promised them $1,200 per month at retirement (with a $600 per month survivor benefit), we would have laid out about $280,000.

I am not arguing that one method of computing pension costs is more "right" than another — merely illustrating that very small changes in wording can have dramatic effects on ultimate costs. The use of "final average pay" versus "career average pay" formulas, the assumption of future salary escalation rates, and the specific benefit percentages selected all interact in ways that can multiply costs manyfold.

In the public sector, the problem has been magnified by officials who grant generous pension benefits (thereby winning the support of employee groups) while leaving the bill to be paid by future taxpayers. These future liabilities are frequently not well understood — and, in many cases, are deliberately obscured. The inflationary environment then multiplies the costs far beyond what anyone contemplated. In effect, these officials have made promises that will be paid out of current and future revenues with interest — effectively functioning as a cost-of-living escalator.

## The "Earthquake Risk"

In the 1920s in Germany, substantial sums were accumulated in advance to meet pension obligations. As inflation accelerated, the real value of these accumulated funds shrank to almost nothing. Today, the pensions paid to German retirees are related to current salary levels of active workers — and are paid out of current revenues of the businesses employing those workers.

The contrast between the past and the present is staggering. The investments that were set aside decades ago became worthless in real terms. Current salaries are thousands of times higher in nominal terms than they were in the 1920s. Pension obligations are now based on these current salaries — and must be backed by the current earnings power of the business, not by assets accumulated decades earlier.

If salaries rise at 25% per year and the pension fund earns 10% per year, the purchasing power of the accumulated fund shrinks relentlessly. Each year, the accumulated fund buys fewer hamburgers — even though it has grown in nominal dollars through accumulated dividends and interest. The fund cannot keep pace with the escalator built into the obligations.

Little attention has been given to this "earthquake risk" — perhaps because it is overshadowed by fears of nuclear war, or perhaps because the mathematical models used by actuaries and financial analysts assume a relatively stable economic environment. Yet companies are assuming open-ended pension obligations without adequate protection against the ravages of sustained inflation, especially for retirees whose benefits are tied to final salaries or cost-of-living adjustments.

Sustained double-digit inflation makes pre-funding of obligations nearly impossible. The situation is not unlike that faced in rubber and aluminum contracts. Social Security faces a similar problem — without the benefit of a tax authority to extract additional revenues from current workers. Future purchasers of the company's products must be willing to pay prices that reflect not just current wages but also promises made to past workers.

## The Investment Management Problem Inherent in All Pension Plans

Because of the danger of substantial attrition in the purchasing power of money, particular caution should be exercised in making pension benefit promises. Even the most careful promises may be affected by economic discontinuities that are far beyond the ability of corporate managers to anticipate or control.

Funding and investment behavior, however, are far from certainties in their impact on outcomes.

In advance funding — the method used by businesses — funds are set aside while employees are productive, with the goal of accumulating enough to purchase an annuity at retirement. This approach requires assumptions about savings account returns, turnover rates, salary escalation, longevity, and other variables. These estimates spread the lifetime cost of pension benefits over the employee's productive years.

(Social Security, by contrast, operates on a pay-as-you-go basis funded by taxes on current workers and their employers. This system works well if the ratio of workers to retirees remains stable, inflation is moderate, and economic growth continues. But it faces severe strain when demographics shift, inflation accelerates, or economic growth stalls — and the problems of one system inevitably exacerbate those of the other. Private plans face additional fall-out from Social Security difficulties.)

Unlike the federal government, a business corporation must create a "savings account" — an investment portfolio that accumulates and invests funds over time to meet future obligations.

## The History of Corporate Pension Plan Management

### Act One — The Awakening

Decades ago, pensions were relatively new and savings accounts were in their formative stages. Promises were smaller and the required retirement annuities were correspondingly modest. Pension fund contributions — the "savings accounts" — were largely forgotten until the awakening of the 1960s.

Several factors converged to bring pension assets to management's attention:

(1) The funds had grown to the point where they sometimes were 25% to 50% of the net worth of the company — often making the assets employed in the savings account larger than those employed in the company's largest division. The following figures are from year-end 1972:

(In Millions)

| Company | Corporate Net Worth | Pension Fund Assets |
|---|---|---|
| A & P | $599 | $236 |
| DuPont | $3,268 | $1,817 |
| Firestone | $1,251 | $423 |
| IBM | $7,565 | $1,023 |
| U. S. Steel | $3,577 | $2,239 |

So, while U. S. Steel had a visible $3.6 billion in net operating assets which management probably spent 99% plus of their business hours thinking about, they had $2.2 billion in the "bank", whose economic results would impact future values for the shareholders, dollar for dollar, with the economic results of the steel assets.

(In fairness to U. S. Steel, it should be mentioned that they were one of the pioneers in recognizing the importance of pension assets — and have done a better-than-average job through in-house management.)

(2) The returns actually realized on the "savings account" had an enormous impact on costs. A change of only 1% in the earning rate could swing the annual cost by as much as 15% for a typical contributing company.

(3) During a period when equities had produced fabulous returns, many pension funds had substantial bond investments earning low fixed rates of 3% to 5%. As interest rates rose, the market values of these bonds shrank, producing the well-known inverse relationship between bond prices and interest rates.

(4) Management, accustomed to evaluating divisional earnings and seeking high returns on operating assets, began to question why $100 million invested in an engine division earning 12% was receiving all the attention while $4 million in a pension fund earning only 4% was being ignored. The question arose: couldn't pension fund returns be improved by applying more effort and taking more risk?

### Act Two — The Great Leap

And so the hunt was on. Wall Street abhors a commercial vacuum. If the will to believe stirs within the customer, the merchandise will be supplied — without warranty. When franchise companies are wanted by investors, franchise companies will be found — and recommended by the underwriters. If there are none to be found, they w.i.l.l be created. Similarly, if above-average investment performance is sought, it will be promised in abundance — and at least the illusion will be produced.

Initially those who know better will resist promising the impossible. As the clientele first begins to drain away, advisors will argue the unsoundness of the new trend and the strengths of the old methods. But when the trickle gives signs of turning into a flood, business Darwinism prevails and organizations adapt.

Banks and corporate managers sought yardsticks for decision-making, favoring MBAs, speed, and recent performance records. The emphasis shifted to finding managers who could demonstrate superior recent results — regardless of whether those results were attributable to skill or luck.

The assumption was that professionally managed money could beat the market. Yet approximately 70% of stock market trading is now conducted by professional money managers — and, collectively, they are the market. It is a logical impossibility for the majority of professionally managed money to outperform the total environment of which they constitute the major part.

Expectations of above-average performance were doomed to disappointment. This was exacerbated by high portfolio turnover rates, which imposed significant transaction costs, and by the difficulty of measuring performance accurately over short periods.

* As an illustration of the expectations now prevailing, in April 1975 an article in the Conference Board magazine described the "Investment Guidelines" statement of a major manufacturing company which explicitly stated its expectation of long-term results superior to the S & P 500 index.

The Becker study provides illuminating data on the actual performance of pension fund managers. Transaction costs — which cannot be measured with precision but certainly must run at least 2% on average when applied to the round trip of purchase and sale — impose a significant drag. A 25% turnover rate among professionals, which is probably conservative, reduces group performance by about 1/2 of 1% per annum ($1½ billion per year on $300 billion of assets). The result is that almost all recent investment management performance by pension funds of large corporations has been fair to poor.

* In the short term, it frequently is better to look smart than to be smart. If you hire a baseball player based on his recent batting record, you may get Joe DiMaggio or Ted Williams — but you may also get a player whose batting style will not produce solid hits going forward. (Harder to apply in investing, however — it is easier to identify a couple of palookas at the bridge table.)

## Is There Hope? Can a Wise Corporation Assure Superior Investment Performance for its Pension Plan?

If you get 1,000 people to flip coins, some will get 10 heads in a row. These "winners" may emerge by chance alone — and after 5 or 10 flips, some will appear successful. But such "success" may be statistically indistinguishable from luck, especially when confirmed only in the marketplace.

Even if some managers succeed modestly through skill, they are unlikely to be identifiable solely by past record. The bare statistical record cannot distinguish them from their lucky peers.

True advantage requires more than just investment knowledge — it demands understanding human personality, selecting managers with proven methods, and having a very wise and informed client who avoids the pitfalls that beset most investors.

Above-average performance is unlikely to be sustained with large sums of managed money. With $20 billion under management, while it could attract top talent, the scale itself may hinder effective management. At $50 million annually in fees on $20 billion, one would expect to assemble: (a) industry experts covering minute developments, (b) top-flight economists tracking market trends, and (c) nimble portfolio managers translating insights into action. It just doesn't work that way.

## Illustrations of Reality in Pension Fund Investment Management

With $20 billion in funds managed by organizations with identical inputs and expert teams, similar results are to be expected due to limited investment opportunities — especially when large sums are involved. Increased funding reduces real investment options.

The rational expectation of "assuring above average pension fund management" is nearly zero.

The following figures are from a pension fund managing approximately $250 million in assets as of June 30, 1975, with a 9½-year record. Three major banks each managed $20–$50 million. Chase's management role was terminated due to poor performance, with an estimated cost impact of $2–$4 million over the period.

**Annual Compounded Rate of Return (including Dividends):**

| Entity | Return |
|---|---|
| Dow-Jones Industrials | +2.8% |
| S & P 500 | +3.8% |
| Continental Illinois | +1.8% |
| First National City | +1.0% |
| Morgan Guaranty | +3.4% |

Security duplication across portfolios was substantial — 64% overlap in Continental's portfolio versus Morgan's, 44% in First National City's, with similar overlaps elsewhere.

The overall record is not dissimilar from the mildly below-average record of the major money managers. Fashion-hitting never has been successfully maintained by managers on Wall Street. High turnover, major mistakes, and furious activity to catch up characterized much of the performance. The pension fund results compared unfavorably to the lumbering, stiff-legged minuet performed by the major banks.

I am reasonably familiar with this fund's record and believe there were no unusual constraints placed on the managers. The relative standing has low predictive value, and Morgan's performance has been above average compared to other large banks and pension funds.

A "Pensions & Investments" magazine article rated the 35 largest banks based on five-year performance records of commingled equity funds. Ten banks performed as well as or better than the S&P — consistent with probability theory if operations were random. Any modest drag is attributable to transaction costs. I know of no statistics that would support an opposing view regarding manager handling of large sums.

The evidence supports the conclusion that above-average investment results should not be expected from conventionally managed corporate pension plans.

## Major Options in Pension Fund Management

There are several paths available for pension fund investments in securities:

(1) **Retain one or more large conventional money managers.** Expect slightly poorer-than-average performance due to costs. Major banks are safe fiduciaries under ERISA, and conventional behavior is acceptable even with potential litigants' benefits of hindsight. If there is no reason to change, stay with Morgan.

(2) **Abandon active security selection** by creating a passive portfolio equivalent to "average," minimizing turnover and management costs. This may achieve results similar to option (1), though with higher potential liability since no effort is being expended to "manage." Several funds have recently duplicated averages explicitly. No management is cheaper, and slightly better than average paid management after transaction costs. This can be implemented through fund participation or do-it-yourself approaches.

(3) **Managers handling smaller amounts** may have good records for the right reasons. However, good records often attract money flows regardless of whether they reflect skill, luck, or even semi-fraudulent activity — and such records tend to wilt under market stress. Identifying superior performers is extremely difficult. Institutional skills that persist despite changing conditions are rare, and "superior" results achieved by individuals in specialized areas (e.g., General Motors, IBM, Sears) may not be replicable in other contexts.

Pension funds' attempts to find unknown managers rely heavily on past record and extensive interview processes. The overall record of such efforts is not encouraging.

* Your win-loss percentage in tennis will not be determined by the absolute level of ability that you possess, but by the opponents you select. The same principle applies to bridge and poker — while harder to apply in investing, it is easier to identify a couple of palookas at the bridge table.

(4) A fixed income (bonds) investment strategy can be followed, currently producing approximately 9% annual returns. This approach is popular, defensible under ERISA, and its attractiveness depends on future equity returns. Many companies made this decision decades ago when rates were 4% rather than 9%. Shifting from bonds to stocks requires a skill possessed by few and is not recommended for most groups — unless one has achieved mastery of this particular form of alchemy.

Comfort levels are high under most conditions, except under high rates of inflation, which would produce distress under all of the options discussed herein.

(5) My final option involves what might be described as a business acquisition approach to portfolio management. This involves a different angle — one that has implications for directors' responsibilities and legal risk under ERISA. The portfolio management decision resembles the business acquisition decisions routinely made by corporate managers.

Company directors consider the long-term attractiveness of specific business operations — judging television businesses, newspapers, and a wide spectrum of other business operations. These judgments cover a broad range of operational characteristics.

When purchasing entire businesses through negotiation, one pays negotiated prices. But buying small pieces of businesses through the stock market has four disadvantages compared to buying entire businesses: (a) no management rights or right to select managers; (b) absence of dividend policy or ability to direct reinvestment; (c) reduced ability to borrow long-term against business assets; and (d) loss of opportunity to sell on a full-value, private-owner basis.

* I am leaving most of the legal discussion out of this memo — partly due to space considerations and partly because it is not my field. However, the advice remains: trust large, well-regarded conventional organizations, and diversify among well-financed enterprises purchased at reasonable valuations.

There are offsetting advantages in private versus public transactions, and no moral stigma should attach to dealing with unwitting buyers.

Volatility in stock markets means that internal rates of return should be measured over time against pension fund results. This approach differs from simply picking stocks — it requires a different attitude, confirmation of judgment, and mental adjustment in evaluating corporate ownership.

I believe that intrinsic business value is the prime determinant of stock prices. As my former boss said: *"In the short run the market is a voting machine, but in the long run it is a weighing machine."* With investments of $12 million or $20 million, generating earnings of $1.5 million, the portion of such earnings paid out to us clearly is worth 100 cents on the dollar.

The returns from reinvesting in similar-value companies may be compared to bond investment returns. This is the concept behind the "pension division" — treating the pension portfolio as a division of the company.

The long-term risks include the possibility that future returns may turn sour. Advantages of this acquisition approach include bargain pricing, flexibility through the public stock market, and the option to delay purchases until favorable conditions arise.

Program success must be measured by earnings and asset growth — not by short-term market fluctuations. The $12 million capitalization is expected to produce better-than-bond returns, and the decision was made to use accumulated stock rather than bonds or common stocks for funding. Implementation decisions should be made only after consulting qualified analysts who understand the unusual selection criteria, and this would be handled infrequently.

## Pension Asset Management in the Scale of Business Priorities

Combinations of policy options are possible — for example, (1) and (3), or (4) and (5). ERISA's exclusive benefit provision must be considered. As the saying goes: *"If a thing's not worth doing at all, it's not worth doing well."* With $1 million in pension assets growing only about $600,000 per year, activities that might incur unwarranted criticism should be avoided.

Contributions directed toward U.S. Steel could project $40 million in direct pension "division" funds within ten years, with earnings from businesses purchased through this "division" running at least $5 million per year.

* In prior years it was felt that by limiting contributions to union plans to so much per shift, or per hour, management was relieved of responsibility for specific benefit levels. This "hands off" position has eroded, and future accountability for promised benefits will be inescapable.

## Summing Up:

(1) In a world of sustained double-digit inflation, private pension plans and the investment process can do little to mitigate such a disaster. Hope lies mainly in the care with which past promises have been made, and in ownership of businesses whose economic characteristics allow pass-through pricing — including labor costs and current costs.

(2) In a more orderly world, the care with which promises have been worded remains important, but on a diminishing scale as inflation moderates. Conventional money management approaches should not be expected to produce above-average results — but average results will be perfectly acceptable at low inflation rates.

(3) A mildly non-conventional investment approach, emphasizing a business approach to security selection, offers some opportunity for long-term results slightly above average without corresponding increase in investment risk.

Warren E. Buffett

October 14, 1975
